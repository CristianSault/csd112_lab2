Scientific Name

Octopus vulgaris

Physiological Info

Octopuses have soft, baggy bodies with eight arms lined with suckers (typically 200-300 per arm). They range greatly in size depending on species—the common octopus averages 12-36 inches (30-90 cm) and weighs 6.6-22 pounds (3-10 kg), while the giant Pacific octopus can reach 16 feet (5 meters) across and weigh up to 110 pounds (50 kg). They have three hearts (two pump blood to the gills, one to the body), blue copper-based blood, a hard beak made of chitin, and no bones or internal skeleton. Their bodies can squeeze through any opening larger than their beak. They possess specialized skin cells called chromatophores that allow them to change color and texture instantly for camouflage and communication.

Natural Habitat

Octopuses are found in oceans worldwide, from shallow tide pools to depths exceeding 13,000 feet (4,000 meters), though most species prefer coastal waters. They inhabit coral reefs, rocky crevices, seagrass beds, and sandy or muddy seafloor environments. Different species occupy different zones—some prefer tropical waters while others live in cold temperate or even Arctic waters. They're solitary creatures that typically establish dens in rock crevices, empty shells, or man-made debris, which they often fortify with rocks and shells. They prefer areas with good hiding spots for protection from predators.

Diet

Octopuses are carnivorous hunters that primarily feed on crustaceans (crabs, shrimp, lobsters), mollusks (clams, snails), and fish. They hunt using several strategies: ambush predation, active pursuit, and foraging. They capture prey with their arms and suckers, then use their sharp beak to break shells or tear flesh. They inject venom through their saliva to paralyze prey and begin digestion. Some species drill holes in shellfish using a specialized radula (tongue-like organ) and acidic secretions. They're intelligent hunters that can learn, remember locations of prey, and solve problems to access food.

Fun Facts

  • They have nine brains—one central brain and a smaller "mini-brain" in each of their eight arms, allowing each arm to act semi-independently and even continue moving after being severed
  • Each arm can taste what it touches through chemoreceptors in the suckers, essentially "tasting" their environment
  • They're considered the most intelligent invertebrates, capable of using tools, solving puzzles, learning through observation, and showing individual personalities
  • They can regenerate lost arms, though the process takes several months
  • Most octopus species live only 1-2 years, and all species die shortly after reproduction (males die within months, females die after their eggs hatch from starvation and exhaustion)
  • They can jet-propel themselves backward by forcefully expelling water through their siphon, reaching speeds of 25 mph (40 km/h) in short bursts
  • When threatened, they can release a cloud of dark ink that obscures their escape and dulls predators' sense of smell
